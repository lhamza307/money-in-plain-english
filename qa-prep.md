# Q&A Prep — Midterm Presentation
# Money in Plain English

**Format:** The 5 hardest investor questions, in order of difficulty. Each includes the honest answer and how to frame it on your feet.

---

## Question 1

### "Your target customer said her magic fix was to 'shop smarter' and 'appreciate what she has.' She didn't ask for an app. She described a mindset shift. Why do you believe she'll download and use a product she never asked for?"

**Why this is dangerous:** This is the sharpest question in the room. It attacks the foundation — not the pricing, not the revenue model, not the technology — but whether anyone actually wants what you're building. And you don't have a clean answer, because you haven't tested it yet.

**The honest answer:**

"You're right, and it's the most important open question in the venture. Noor did not ask for a product. She described a values and behavioral shift she wants to make — not a tool. I haven't yet put the product concept directly in front of a target customer and watched their reaction, which means I'm still one step away from knowing whether the form factor matches the need.

What I can say is this: users of Mint and Credit Karma also didn't walk around asking for auto-categorized spending data — but when Mint showed them the feature, they signed up by the millions. The behavior Noor describes — overspending after payday, vague resolve, cycle repeating — is a real problem. Whether a weekly summary is the right intervention for it is a hypothesis I haven't confirmed.

My immediate plan: the next round of interviews will describe the product concept explicitly and observe her reaction, not ask for a stated preference. If she asks a follow-up question about the product before reacting to the price, that's a yes signal. If she shrugs, that's the answer. I need that data before I build anything."

**What not to say:** Do not say Mint proved this works. Mint shut down. Do not reference success stories from companies that had $30M in marketing budget and a decade of brand trust.

## Question 2

### "Your entire product requires users to hand over their bank credentials to an unknown, early-stage product. You've done 2 interviews and never asked either person whether they'd do that. What makes you confident this isn't a fatal barrier?"

**Why this is dangerous:** Willingness to connect a bank account is the single structural assumption the entire product rests on. If users won't do it, there is no product. And you have zero data on it.

**The honest answer:**

"I don't have confidence — I have an assumption I haven't tested. Neither Noor nor Ben was asked whether they'd share bank credentials with a new product. That was an omission I've since identified as the most critical gap in my validation work.

The risk is real. Plaid publishes connection rates, and early-stage fintech products typically see significant drop-off at the bank-connection step — users who sign up but abandon before linking accounts. I don't know what my drop-off rate would be.

What I know is that Plaid exists because users do connect accounts at scale when they trust the product. The question for me is whether I can build enough trust, fast enough, for a user like Noor to take that step before she's experienced any value.

The bank account question is the first thing I'm asking in every customer interview going forward. Specifically: 'Would you connect your bank account to an app you'd just heard about? What would make you comfortable or uncomfortable with that?' The answer will tell me whether this is a fatal barrier or a friction point I can design around — and I'll know within two weeks."

**What not to say:** Do not say "Plaid makes it easy." Plaid makes the technical connection easy. Trust is a different problem entirely.

## Question 3

### "Your own model shows 48 months to break-even, burning $6,578 a month. That's over $300,000 in losses before sustainability. Where does that money come from — and what happens if the institutional sales cycle runs longer than 18 months, as your own stress test shows it might?"

**Why this is dangerous:** This is a math problem, not a vision problem. The model is internally consistent and it shows a capital requirement the venture cannot currently meet. An investor who does the arithmetic before the Q&A will have this ready.

**The honest answer:**

"The model is honest about the gap. At $3 PMPM and one institutional contract per quarter, I reach break-even in 48 months. At 20% annual churn and the same pace, it's 60 months. Neither of those is a viable path without capital or a structurally different approach.

I see three ways to change this, in order of what I can control:

First, the base case PMPM assumption may be wrong. If institutional buyers actually pay $6 instead of $3 — which the unvalidated competitive data suggests is possible — break-even drops to 21 months at 7 contracts. That's a meaningful difference. The pricing test happening this week will tell me which case is closer to reality.

Second, the consumer-first sequencing may be the wrong order. The version of the model that most directly survives contact with reality is direct B2B2C from day one — sell to one institution first, let them distribute to users, skip the consumer acquisition phase entirely. That eliminates the cash gap between free users and first institutional revenue. The tradeoff is that I'm building for the institution's needs first, not Noor's.

Third, and honestly: this model requires outside capital if the current sequencing holds. I don't have that capital today, and I'm not going to pretend I do. The venture is pre-product, pre-revenue, and pre-seed. The right question for the next stage is what milestones — a signed letter of intent from one institution, a specific number of free users with engagement data — would justify a raise and at what size."

**What not to say:** Do not say "revenue will come faster than the model shows." That's a guess. The model already has optimistic assumptions baked in.

## Question 4

### "Plaid has the infrastructure. Mint had this same idea and shut down with millions of users. YNAB, Monarch, and Credit Karma all have the transaction data. What stops any of them from shipping a 'plain English weekly summary' feature next quarter? What do you have that they can't copy?"

**Why this is dangerous:** This is the moat question, and right now you don't have one. You're a student at a venture creation course with no product, no users, no IP, and no defensible advantage. Every word you say here will either be honest or it will be a red flag.

**The honest answer:**

"Honestly, right now: nothing structural. No patent, no proprietary data, no network effects, no switching costs, no exclusive partnerships. Any of the competitors you named could ship this feature. Some of them may already be working on it.

What I'd push back on is the assumption that being first to ship a feature wins. Mint had every major bank's data, 20 million users, and Intuit's distribution behind it — and still couldn't keep users engaged. The category problem isn't access to data. It's engagement after the first impression. Every existing tool loses users because they ask for ongoing effort the user won't give.

The bet I'm making is that category leadership in pre-budgeting — the step before formal financial management — goes to whoever builds the most trusted, lowest-friction relationship with users at the moment they become financially independent. That's not a product feature. It's a brand position built on consistent, honest communication over time.

That's a race, not a moat. I have no advantage today. If I can get to 10,000 engaged users before the category becomes crowded — with institutional data to prove engagement — that's a defensible position. If I can't, you're right that I'm building something anyone can replicate.

The honest version of the moat question for me right now is: can I get to meaningful scale before incumbents prioritize this segment? I don't know yet."

**What not to say:** Do not say "the AI layer is proprietary." It isn't. Do not say incumbents are too slow to move. They aren't — Credit Karma shipped a credit score feature in 90 days when a competitor appeared.

## Question 5

### "Your revenue model depends on HR directors and university administrators paying $3–6 per member per month. You've never spoken to an institutional buyer. The vendors you named — LearnLux, SmartDollar, Financial Finesse — have SOC 2 certifications, existing relationships, and sales teams. Why would a procurement officer choose an unknown early-stage product with no users, no compliance record, and no track record over a vendor they already know?"

**Why this is dangerous:** This is the full weight of the B2B2C model landing on someone who has never sold B2B anything. Institutional procurement is not a market you can enter without either a champion inside the organization, a dramatically better product at a dramatically lower price, or a specific segment that incumbents are actively underserving.

**The honest answer:**

"A procurement officer at a large employer probably wouldn't choose me over an established vendor — not today, and not without a product or user base. You're right. I'm not the right fit for that buyer at this stage.

The institutional buyer I can actually reach is different: a small university financial aid office, a community college student success program, or an early-career employee resource group at a forward-thinking mid-size employer. These buyers are not the same as the enterprise buyers that LearnLux is closing. They have smaller budgets, shorter procurement cycles, and a genuine gap in what's available for the specific segment I'm targeting — students and employees in their first 1–3 years who aren't yet served by tools designed for financially established users.

My realistic first institutional customer is probably Rollins College itself. The financial aid office already knows me. I'm building for students who look exactly like Rollins graduates. The ask is a pilot — 20 to 50 users, no money, in exchange for engagement data and a reference. That's a door I can open without a sales team or a SOC 2 certification.

From there, I need one signed reference before I approach the next institution. The path isn't 'sell to enterprise from day one.' It's 'pilot with one institution that already trusts me, prove the product works, then use that data to open the next door.'

What I still don't know: whether even the small institutional buyer is accessible without SOC 2. That's a real cost — $15,000 to $40,000 per year — and I can't currently say when I'd clear it or how. That's the compliance question I need to answer before I can make the B2B2C path credible to any buyer."

**What not to say:** Do not say you'll hire a sales team. Do not say you'll get SOC 2 "quickly." Do not say large enterprises will move fast for a good product.

## How to Use This in the Room

**If you get one of these questions:** Take a breath before answering. Investors respect a founder who thinks before speaking more than one who rushes to sound confident.

**On "I don't know" answers:** You will give several of those. Frame each one as: here is what I don't know, here is why I don't know it yet, and here is the specific thing I am doing to find out. Uncertainty framed as a plan is competence. Uncertainty framed as optimism is a red flag.

**The question you most want:** "What would you do with a check?" You don't have a fundraise planned yet and that's fine to say — but have a one-sentence answer ready: *"At this stage, the most valuable capital is introductions to institutional buyers and more target customer interviews. When I have pricing validation and a signed letter of intent, I'll know what size check makes the model work."*
