# Interview Synthesis

Two interviews conducted. One confirmed target customer, one explicitly outside the target.

---

## Interview 1 — Ben, Age 36

**Who he is and his situation:**
Ben is 36, married, and has been managing his own finances for years. He splits spending across two accounts: one for shared household expenses (food, gas, tolls, mortgage) and one for personal spending. He uses Bank of America's native mobile banking tools to track the personal account. He is actively saving toward a vacation home with a goal of paying it off in 20 years or less. Laila noted him as "not my target customer."

**Key quotes:**
- "I pay for all of my bills out of one account and my personal expenses out of another account. Only a certain amount goes to my personal account and my expenses are all tracked through Bank of America's mobile banking tools."
- "I don't like talking to my wife about setting the budget. I like that I know how much money I have to spend personally."
- "Very confident. Don't really struggle tracking any categories at all."
- "Automated saving or allotment toward goals anytime spending is below budget."

**What surprised or challenged assumptions:**
Ben built a clean, functional system entirely on his own using a free bank app and two accounts — no dedicated budgeting tool. His system works, and he knows it works. His one pain point isn't awareness or tracking; it's the friction of budget conversations with his spouse. His magic fix isn't about understanding his money better — it's about automating action toward a goal once the money is already under control. This suggests that once someone reaches financial confidence, what they want next is goal-directed automation, not more visibility. That's a different product than what's being built.

## Interview 2 — Noor, Age 24

**Who she is and her situation:**
Noor is 24, a recent master's graduate who spent time unable to find a job and was financially supported by her parents and partner. She recently started a job in her field and is now becoming financially independent. She is preparing to move out soon and is managing student loans, credit cards, and everyday expenses (gas, etc.) simultaneously. She is the closest match to the target customer so far.

**Key quotes:**
- "As of currently, I am trying to manage my money as I am moving out soon. I am careful with my spending, and I double think purchases over, whether they're necessary or not."
- "Yes, there have been many moments. I would say this happens after I get paid and purchase items I had previously wanted but did not need."
- "I would try and be more careful with my money following that realization."
- "The sheer amount of it. On top of saving money to move out, I also have other expenses such as credit cards, gas, etc."
- "To shop smarter. I definitely need to re-evaluate purchases and appreciate what I already have, in terms of food, cosmetics, clothes, etc."

**What surprised or challenged assumptions:**

The venture hypothesis is that the core problem is a lack of spending awareness — that people don't know where their money goes and need a tool to show them. Noor complicates this.

She is already aware. She double-thinks purchases consciously. She has experienced the surprise spending moment and recognized it in real time. When asked what she did after discovering she had overspent, she didn't say she looked for a tool or tried to build a budget — she said she would "try and be more careful." The awareness is present. The sustained behavioral change is not.

Her magic fix confirms this: she didn't ask for better visibility into her spending. She asked to "shop smarter" and to "re-evaluate purchases" and to "appreciate what I already have." That is a self-discipline and mindset framing, not a technology or information framing. She is describing a behavioral problem, not an awareness problem.

This challenges the assumption that visibility alone changes behavior — which is also the explicit risk flagged under Concept 1 (Money in Plain English) in the competitive analysis.

---

## Across Both Interviews

**What holds:**
- Noor confirms the spending-surprise moment is real and happens to the target customer.
- Noor has no formal system — she is operating on intention alone ("try and be more careful"), which is weak.
- The financial pressures she describes (student loans, moving expenses, credit cards) match the problem statement exactly.

**What is under pressure:**
- Neither interviewee asked for an app, a financial coach, or AI-powered categorization. Neither named a tool they wished existed.
- The target customer (Noor) already has partial awareness — her gap is not seeing the data, it is changing behavior after seeing it. The proposed solution focuses on the awareness step, but the evidence so far suggests the harder and more valuable step is what comes after awareness.
- Ben's system (two accounts, one native bank app) is effective and free. It raises the question of whether the problem requires a new product at all, or whether it requires a better on-ramp to habits people could build themselves.

**Sample size caveat:**
Two interviews is not enough to validate or invalidate anything. The patterns above are worth tracking, not concluding from.
