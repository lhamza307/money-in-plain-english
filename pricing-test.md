# Pricing Test Design
**Designed:** June 2026
**Test window:** This week (June 24–28, 2026)
**Context:** No product exists. No buyer has been approached. Every price in the financial model is an assumption. This test replaces the most important assumption before anything else is built.

---

## Why institutional pricing, not consumer pricing

The financial model has two unvalidated price points:

- **Consumer:** Would Noor pay $4.99–$9.99/month? Unknown.
- **Institutional:** Will an employer or university pay $6/month per employee/student? Unknown. This number drives every break-even calculation in the model.

The consumer test feels more familiar. Test it second. The institutional test is worth ten times as much — a single real number from one buyer replaces the assumption that determines whether the entire business model works. That is where this week goes.

The consumer test is included below as a secondary test for the same week — it takes one extra interview question and costs nothing to add.

---

## Pricing Hypothesis

### Primary (institutional)

> "An HR benefits manager or director at a mid-size employer (200–1,000 employees) will pay **$5–8 per employee per month** for a financial wellness tool targeting employees in their first 1–3 years of employment, because comparable programs — LearnLux, SmartDollar, Financial Finesse — already exist in this budget line, and financial stress among early-career employees is a documented HR concern tied to retention and productivity."

**What this hypothesis needs to survive:**
- At least one HR buyer confirms a budget line for financial wellness benefits
- The price they describe for current programs overlaps with $5–8 PMPM
- They do not immediately disqualify the product category

**What kills it:**
- Every buyer says they pay $0–1 PMPM (price is wrong by 5–8x)
- No buyer has a budget line for this category at all (market doesn't exist at employer level)
- Every buyer says decisions take 2+ years and require 3 procurement approvals (timing kills the model regardless of price)

### Secondary (consumer)

> "A recent college graduate who recognizes she overspends will say 'I'd try that' or ask a follow-up question when shown a $4.99/month price for automatic, no-setup spending awareness — not because she has been price-validated before, but because the value framing addresses the exact moment she described: knowing she overspent and not knowing what to do next."

This is a reaction test, not a commitment test. A raised eyebrow is data. A shrug is data. "I'd just use Mint" is data.

---

## Test Method: Direct Ask (Both Tests)

**Why not a smoke test or landing page:**
Institutional buyers do not sign up on landing pages. They respond to direct, honest outreach from a founder who is explicitly asking for their expertise, not their money. A landing page targeting HR managers with no product behind it is likely to be ignored and cannot teach you pricing.

**Why not a Wizard of Oz:**
There is no product to simulate yet, and no consumer has asked for one. Wizard of Oz works when you have a customer who wants the thing — here the customer does not yet know they want it. Direct conversation reveals that faster.

**Why not a pre-sale:**
A pre-sale requires enough trust to take money. That trust has not been built. No consumer interview has been completed since Week 4, and the product concept has never been placed in front of a target customer. Pre-selling before the concept has been validated confuses two separate tests.

**Why direct ask:**
Both buyer types — HR managers and target consumers — are reachable this week through your existing network. The institutional ask requires 15 minutes of their time and no product. The consumer ask adds two questions to an interview you should be running anyway. The cost is zero. The return is the most important number in your model.

## Step-by-Step Plan

### TEST 1: Institutional Pricing (Primary)

**Goal:** Determine what employers actually pay per employee per month for financial wellness programs — and whether your $6 base case is in range, too high, or too low.

---

**Tuesday, June 24 (today) — Identify targets**

Find 5 people in your network who are in or adjacent to HR or benefits. Sources:

- LinkedIn: search your connections for titles — "HR Manager," "HR Director," "Benefits Manager," "Total Rewards," "People Operations," "HR Business Partner." Mid-size companies (200–1,000 employees) are more likely to have a financial wellness budget than small employers.
- Family members or family friends who work in HR at any company.
- Rollins alumni network: search LinkedIn for Rollins grads in HR roles.
- Career center staff at Rollins — they interface with employer HR contacts regularly and may be able to make an introduction.
- Professors: ask your entrepreneurship or business professors if they know anyone in HR or benefits who would give 15 minutes.

Target: 5 names by end of day Tuesday. You do not need 5 meetings — you need 5 outreach attempts to get 2–3 meetings.

---

**Tuesday evening — Draft outreach message**

Use this exact framing. Do not say "I'm building a startup" — say you are a student doing research. This is true and gets more candid responses.

> "Hi [Name], I'm a student at Rollins College working on a venture project focused on financial wellness for early-career employees. I'm trying to understand how employers think about financial wellness as a benefit — what programs exist, what they cost, and where the gaps are. I'm not pitching anything. I'd love 15 minutes of your time to ask a few questions. Would you have time this week or early next week?"

Send this via LinkedIn message or email. Personalize the first sentence if you have a mutual connection. Do not attach anything. Keep it under 5 sentences.

---

**Wednesday, June 25 — Send outreach, book university side**

- Send the message to all 5 people identified Tuesday.
- Separately, contact the financial aid office at Rollins or another local university. Ask to speak with someone about financial wellness resources for students. You are not pitching — you want to understand what programs they currently use and what they pay. Frame it as research.

Target: 2 confirmed meetings by end of Wednesday, either with employers or with the university contact.

---

**Thursday, June 26 — Conduct 2 buyer conversations (15 min each)**

Run each conversation with the same script. Do not deviate. The point is to hear their words, not to explain your idea.

**Opening (2 minutes):**
> "Thanks for making time. I'm designing a financial wellness tool for early-career employees — people in their first 1–3 years of their first real job. Before I build anything, I want to understand what already exists on the employer side. Can I ask you a few questions?"

**Question 1 (listen, do not talk):**
> "What financial wellness resources or programs does your company currently offer employees, if any?"

Write down every word they say. Do not interpret. Do not react.

**Question 2 (the pricing question — ask this regardless of their answer to Q1):**
> "For programs like that — or programs you've seen — do you have a sense of what employers typically pay per employee per month?"

If they don't know: "Even a rough range would be helpful — like, is it dollars per month, or cents per month, or something else entirely?"

**Question 3 (if they confirm a budget line exists):**
> "Is there a gap in what's available — something you'd want to offer but haven't found a good solution for yet?"

**Question 4 (concept reaction — only if the conversation is going well):**
> "I'm designing something for employees in their first 1–3 years who don't yet have a financial system. It would automatically connect to their bank, categorize their spending, and send them a plain-language weekly summary — no setup required. If something like that existed and cost around $6 per employee per month, would that be in range for how your company thinks about this?"

Do not explain the product further. Let them respond. Write down their first reaction word for word.

**Closing:**
> "This has been really helpful. Is there anyone else in HR or benefits you'd suggest I speak with?"

---

**Friday, June 27 — Document and update the model**

Write down the answers to these specific questions from each conversation:

1. Do they currently spend anything on financial wellness? (Yes/No)
2. If yes, what do they pay per employee per month? (Record the number)
3. What product or program are they using?
4. What was their reaction to $6 PMPM — too high, reasonable, too low, or unclear?
5. Did they identify a gap or unmet need?

Open `financial-model.xlsx`. In Section 1, update the institutional PMPM assumptions (rows 8) to reflect what you actually heard. If every buyer says $1–2, the base case drops. If buyers say $8–10, the base case is conservative. Either way, the model now reflects reality instead of inference.

---

### TEST 2: Consumer Pricing (Secondary — same week)

**Goal:** Observe a target customer's first reaction to a specific price for the product concept. This is not a commitment test. You are collecting a reaction, not closing a sale.

---

**Wednesday or Thursday — Schedule one consumer interview**

Reach out to someone who fits the Noor profile: 22–27, recent graduate, working their first real job, managing their own rent and student loans, no formal financial system. This is different from the HR contacts — this is a peer or someone a peer can introduce you to.

Text or message: "Hey — I'm working on a project for class and need to talk to someone about how they manage money. Would you have 20 minutes this week? Coffee on me if you're local."

**During the interview — add two questions at the end**

After 10–15 minutes of discovery questions (what does their current financial situation look like, what happens when they overspend, etc.), say:

> "I want to show you something and get your reaction. [Describe the concept clearly:] 'Imagine an app that connects to your bank account, automatically sorts your spending into categories, and every week sends you a plain-language summary of where your money went — no spreadsheet, no setup, no budget required. You just get a weekly snapshot.' If that existed and cost $4.99 a month — about the price of one coffee — what's your reaction?"

Then stop talking. Watch their face. Write down the first thing they say verbatim.

Follow-up: "What would make you more or less willing to pay for something like that?"

Do not defend the product. Do not explain how it works. Do not negotiate the price down. Just listen.

## What a YES Signal Looks Like vs. a NO Signal

### Institutional test — YES signals

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| "Yes, we have a budget line for financial wellness." | Category exists at this employer. Your market is real. |
| "We pay [number close to $5–8 PMPM] for [comparable program]." | Your base case PMPM is in range. Model holds. |
| "We'd like to do more in this space — there's a gap for newer employees." | Demand exists. You may have identified a segment gap. |
| "That price range sounds reasonable." | Price is not the objection. Move to product and timeline. |
| "Can you send me more information?" or "I'd want to see a demo." | Warm lead. Follow up within 24 hours. |

### Institutional test — NO signals

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| "We don't have a budget for this category." | Either the category doesn't exist at this employer, or your contact is not the right buyer. Ask who makes these decisions. |
| "We pay [number far below $5 PMPM] for what we use." | Your price is too high for institutional buyers. Drop the base case and rebuild break-even. |
| "Our employees don't ask for this." | Demand problem — HR won't spend on something employees haven't requested. |
| "This would take 18 months to get approved." | Your sales cycle assumption is right or conservative. Flag this in the model. |
| "We use [a free tool / bank's native app] and it's enough." | Comparable to Ben's self-built system — validates that some employers solve this without a product. |

### Consumer test — YES signals

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| First reaction is a follow-up question about the product, not the price. | Price is not a blocker. They're interested in the value. |
| "That's cheaper than Netflix / Spotify / [anything they pay for]." | Price anchored to existing subscriptions. Willingness exists. |
| "Yeah, I'd try that." | Stated willingness. Weak signal — stated intent ≠ behavior, but it's something. |
| Price is mentioned second, after product questions. | They're evaluating the product first. |

### Consumer test — NO signals

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| "I'd just use the free version." | Confirms freemium behavior. Freemium acquisition works; B2C subscription doesn't. Does not break the B2B2C model — confirms it. |
| Pause + silence + "I don't know." | Price is an obstacle. The value didn't land before the price did. |
| "I use [Mint / Credit Karma / bank app] — it's free." | Free competitors are the perceived alternative. Need to differentiate on value, not price. |
| "I'd want to try it first." | No upfront payment, but open to a trial. Freemium is the right entry, not a subscription. |
| "$4.99 seems like a lot for an app." | Consumer price sensitivity. Confirms B2C subscription is a hard sell; B2B2C route is the right model. |

## If the Test Fails — Plan B

### If institutional buyers say the price is too high ($1–3 PMPM is the actual range)

This does not kill the venture — it recalibrates it.

- Update the PMPM assumption in `financial-model.xlsx` to $3 (conservative scenario becomes base case).
- Break-even now requires ~13 contracts instead of 7. The model survives but the runway gap grows.
- Consider whether university buyers are a better initial target — shorter contract cycles, more mission-aligned, more likely to pilot with a small cohort.
- Run a second round of institutional conversations specifically with university financial aid offices to test whether the $3 university price is real and achievable faster than employer contracts.

### If no institutional buyers have a budget for this category

This is a signal, not a verdict — two conversations are not a market study.

- Ask each buyer: "What would have to be true for a company like yours to offer something like this?" This gives you the conditions for the market to exist, not just the current state.
- Shift focus to universities. University financial aid staff already have a mandate to help students navigate financial transitions. The product fits their mission more naturally than employer HR. Call Rollins' own financial aid office first — it is the easiest door to open.
- If both employer and university channels produce nothing after 5 conversations, revisit the direct B2B2C-from-day-one strategy flagged in `week5-progress.md`: skip consumer acquisition entirely and sell to one institution first. That means building for the institution's needs, not Noor's.

### If you cannot get any institutional meetings this week

Do not wait. Use the time differently:

- Call LearnLux, SmartDollar, or Financial Finesse and ask for a sales demo, posing as someone evaluating options for a small employer. Their sales rep will quote you a price. That price is real institutional pricing data — better than any estimate in the model.
- Ask your Rollins entrepreneurship professor to make one introduction to an HR contact they know. One warm introduction is worth 20 cold LinkedIn messages.
- Search for public RFPs (Request for Proposals) that universities or employers have posted for financial wellness vendors. These are often public documents and list budget ranges.

### If the consumer test produces a strong NO (she would definitely not pay)

This is the expected result and it does not break anything.

- It confirms that freemium is the right consumer entry point, not subscription.
- Ask the follow-up: "If your employer offered this as a free benefit, would you use it?" If yes, you have just confirmed the B2B2C logic from the user's perspective. Write that down — it is a data point for your institutional pitch.
- Do not revise the model based on this. The model already assumes $0 consumer revenue. A consumer NO is already priced in.

## What This Test Does Not Tell You

This test does not validate whether anyone will actually use the product — only whether institutional buyers have a budget line and what price range it covers, and whether a consumer reacts to a price without flinching. Both are inputs to the model, not proof of product-market fit.

The test also does not resolve whether willingness to connect a bank account — the most critical structural assumption in the entire venture — is there. That question needs to be asked in the consumer interview this week in addition to the pricing question: "Would you connect your bank account to an app like this? What would make you comfortable or uncomfortable with that?"

Add that question. The answer matters as much as the pricing answer.
