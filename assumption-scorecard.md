# Assumption Scorecard

Five interviews conducted. Three confirmed target customers (Noor, 24; Tamara, 24; Emily, 22). One candidate target customer whose life-stage fit is unconfirmed (Dooney, 26). One explicitly outside the target (Ben, 36). Verdicts reflect that sample size — nothing here is settled, though the awareness-vs-behavior-change question is now at the low end of this project's own three-to-five interview threshold.

---

## Assumption 1: Traditional budgeting feels overwhelming or restrictive.

**Evidence for it:**
- Noor has no formal budgeting system. She is managing money on intention alone ("I am careful with my spending, and I double think purchases over"). She did not describe trying a traditional budget and abandoning it, but the absence of any system suggests she has not built one.
- Noor's response after overspending is vague and unstructured: "I would try and be more careful with my money following that realization." There is no mention of sitting down with a budget, which could suggest avoidance — though she was never asked directly.

**Evidence against it:**
- Neither interviewee said traditional budgeting felt overwhelming or restrictive. The word does not appear in either transcript.
- Ben's system functions like a budget — fixed allocation between two accounts — and he reports zero friction with it: "Very confident. Don't really struggle tracking any categories at all." He built it himself without any dedicated tool.
- Noor never described trying traditional budgeting and finding it hard. She may simply have never tried it. Her stated problem is impulse spending after payday, not a failed attempt at a budgeting method.

**Evidence against it, added from Interview 5 (Emily):**
- Emily states her core flaw directly: "no urge to budget, not lack of awareness." She does not describe budgeting as overwhelming, restrictive, or effortful — she describes never having felt motivated to start, despite acknowledging her savings goal is achievable. This is a different mechanism than overwhelm: not "I tried and it was too much," and not "I never thought about it," but "I know what to do and haven't wanted to."

**Verdict: Inconclusive, and now leaning slightly against.**
The assumption was never directly tested with a "have you tried budgeting" question. No interviewee has described budgeting itself as overwhelming or restrictive — three interviewees (Noor, Tamara, Emily) describe not having a system, for three different stated or implied reasons (never tried, relies on someone else, no motivation), none of which is "I tried it and it felt like too much."

**What to change:**
Ask directly in the next round: "Have you ever tried to create a budget or use a budgeting app? Walk me through what happened." Without that, this assumption remains open — and the accumulating evidence (Emily especially) suggests "overwhelming" may not be the right word for the barrier at all.

## Assumption 2: People need awareness before they can change financial behavior.

**Evidence for it:**
- Noor experiences spending-surprise moments that temporarily shift her behavior: "I would try and be more careful with my money following that realization." The realization — the awareness — is what triggers the intention to change. Without it, she continues the pattern.
- The surprise moment itself follows a specific trigger: "I would say this happens after I get paid and purchase items I had previously wanted but did not need." She is not aware in real time — the awareness arrives after the fact, and the behavior repeats next cycle.

**Evidence against it:**
- Noor already has partial, active awareness before any product intervenes. She consciously double-thinks purchases. She knows she overspends after payday. She can describe the pattern clearly. And yet the behavior does not change. This means awareness is present in her case and is still not enough.
- Noor's magic fix is not "show me my spending." It is: "To shop smarter. I definitely need to re-evaluate purchases and appreciate what I already have, in terms of food, cosmetics, clothes, etc." She is describing a values and mindset shift, not an information gap. She does not believe that more awareness is what she needs.
- Ben's magic fix is "automated saving or allotment toward goals anytime spending is below budget." He is already fully aware. What he wants is action taken on his behalf, not more data. This suggests that awareness is a phase people move through, not the permanent gap.

**Evidence for it, added from Interview 3 (Tamara):**
- Tamara rates her own money management at 3/10 and describes having no real experience managing money independently. She has never created a personal budget and reports no anxiety about overspending — not because she has it under control, but because she does not appear to track it closely enough to register the surprise moments Noor describes. This looks like a genuine visibility gap, not a behavior-change gap.

**Evidence against it, added from Interview 5 (Emily):**
- Emily rates her own money management at 6/10 and says directly that she knows where her money goes and dislikes some of the destinations. She names her own core flaw explicitly: "no urge to budget, not lack of awareness." Unlike Noor and Dooney, this isn't inferred from a mismatch between self-image and behavior — she states the conclusion herself. This is the most direct evidence yet against awareness being the gap for at least a meaningful share of the target segment.

**Verdict: Still split, but now weighted — two of three confirmed target-customer interviews lean toward behavior-change-after-awareness.**
Noor already has partial awareness and still doesn't sustain change. Emily has explicit awareness and explicitly lacks the motivation to act on it. Both argue against awareness being the full explanation. Tamara reports no anxiety about overspending and has never budgeted at all — evidence that at least some target customers may genuinely lack basic awareness first. Three confirmed target-customer interviews (the low end of this project's own three-to-five threshold) now split 2-to-1 toward behavior-change-after-awareness. This is meaningful directional weight, not a resolution — the segment may still contain two distinct profiles rather than one dominant pattern, consistent with the Filter A / Filter B split flagged in `business-model-canvas.md`. Counting Dooney's candidate interview (also leaning behavior-change via a self-image/behavior mismatch) makes it 3-to-1 across all target-adjacent interviews, though his life-stage fit is still unconfirmed.

**What to change:**
The next one or two interviews are now genuinely decisive for this question, not just additional color — three confirmed interviews is the low end of the threshold this project has held itself to. Keep asking early: can this person say, with reasonable specificity, what they spent last month in their top two or three categories? A confident, specific answer that isn't acted on looks like Noor or Emily — the gap is behavior change. A vague or absent answer looks like Tamara — the gap is closer to visibility. If the next interview also lands on the behavior-change side, this crosses from "weighted" to "confirmed" by the project's own bar.

## Assumption 3: An AI-based experience would feel easier than a traditional budgeting app.

**Evidence for it:**
- Interview 3 (Tamara): when the concept was described directly — an app that auto-tracks spending and sends weekly summaries — she reacted positively and said she'd pay $5–8/month for it. She specifically named the fully passive, no-manual-input design as the reason it appealed to her.
- Interview 4 (Dooney): reacted positively to a bank-linked tracker concept (auto-categorization, pie charts, cut-back suggestions) and named $15–20/month — the highest price point in the project so far.
- Interview 5 (Emily): reacted positively to a third concept (auto-tracking, weekly summary, overspend flags, margin-adjustment suggestions, light setup) and named ~$10/month, benchmarked to Spotify. She said she would sign up today if the product existed — the strongest stated purchase intent collected so far. She also asked for a feature no prior concept included: proactive, forward-looking nudges, not just a retrospective summary.

**Evidence against it:**
- Neither Ben nor Noor referenced AI at any point, unprompted or in response to any question.
- Tamara's positive reaction came only after the concept was described to her directly — not from an open-ended question about her problem. Stated interest in a described hypothetical is a weaker signal than unprompted demand.
- Tamara also named ChatGPT Plus, which she already pays for, as a substitute she could turn to for similar help. This means the appeal isn't the AI label by itself — a generic AI assistant is already perceived by at least one target customer as a plausible alternative.
- **None of the three positive reactions (Tamara, Dooney, Emily) can be merged into one confirming data point.** Each was shown a different concept — passive-only, dashboard-plus-suggestions, and light-setup-plus-flags, respectively — so three different prices answer three different questions, not one willingness-to-pay range for "Money in Plain English" specifically.

**Verdict: Three thin, non-comparable signals — still not confirmed, but the pattern is starting to look like a gradient rather than scatter.**
This assumption went untested through the first two interviews. Three target-ish customers have now reacted positively to a direct description of a concept and named a price. The new element with Emily: her price ($10) sits between Tamara's ($5–8) and Dooney's ($15–20), and her concept's richness (light setup + suggestions, no dashboard) also sits between theirs. That ordering — richer concept, higher stated price — held across all three data points, which is the first time this hasn't looked like noise. It is still not a controlled test: three different people, three different concepts, no same-concept comparison yet.

**What to change:**
Before running this test again, use the same concept description every time — ideally the exact language in `pricing-test.md`'s consumer script — so future price reactions are comparable to each other. Keep asking the ChatGPT-differentiation follow-up in every remaining interview. If a future interview reacting to the same fixed concept lands close to where the emerging gradient predicts (roughly $5–8 for zero-suggestion passive, ~$10 for light-suggestion, $15–20 for full dashboard-plus-coaching), that would upgrade the gradient from suggestive to real. Also worth testing directly: whether Emily's requested real-time nudge layer is itself a price driver, separate from suggestion richness.

## Summary

| Assumption | Verdict |

| People need awareness before they can change financial behavior | Split but weighted — Noor, Emily, and Dooney's cases argue against a pure awareness gap (all self-aware or admittedly-aware, all still overspend or fail to act); Tamara's argues for one. Two of three confirmed target-customer interviews (three of four counting Dooney) now lean toward behavior-change-after-awareness — at the low end of this project's own confirmation threshold |
| An AI-based experience would feel easier than a traditional budgeting app | Three non-comparable positive signals (Tamara, Dooney, Emily) — three different concepts shown, three different prices named ($5–8 / ~$10 / $15–20) that now form a directional gradient with feature richness rather than pure scatter, but still not a controlled comparison. A free substitute (ChatGPT Plus) was also named by Tamara in the same breath |

No assumption is fully confirmed. Ben and Noor described their problem in behavioral and emotional terms, not as a need for a new tool. Tamara, Dooney, and Emily are all willing to react favorably and name a price once a tool is described — but they reacted to three different tools, so the pattern is "people will engage with a described concept and name a number that roughly scales with what the concept does," not yet "this concept, at this price."

**The awareness-vs-behavior-change question, stated directly instead of inferred:** Emily says outright that her core flaw is "no urge to budget, not lack of awareness." This is the most direct evidence yet against pure awareness-gap framing — not inferred from a mismatch (as with Noor and Dooney), but self-diagnosed. It pushes the confirmed-target-customer split to 2-to-1 toward behavior-change-after-awareness (Noor, Emily) versus 1 (Tamara).

This is the third pricing data point in the project, and the first time the three numbers ($5–8, ~$10, $15–20) line up with concept richness into a directional gradient rather than looking scattered.

**Purchase intent:** Said she would sign up today if the product existed — the strongest stated commitment collected in any interview so far, stronger than Tamara's or Dooney's stated willingness to pay.

**Bank account connection:** Not addressed in this interview. The sample on this specific question remains at two (Tamara, Dooney), unchanged.

**New product-shape input:** Explicitly wants forward-looking, real-time spending nudges ("hey girl, slow down") in addition to a retrospective summary. No prior concept — Tamara's, Dooney's, or the "Money in Plain English" core positioning — included this. Worth testing directly in future interviews as its own feature, separate from suggestion richness.

**Channel signal:** Twitter-primary, follows no personal finance creators, recognizes Shark Tank figures for money quotes. Second interview data point (after Dooney) running against `channel-experiments.md`'s core #FinTok/TikTok-Instagram assumption — this time on a different platform, which broadens the concern.
