Section 3 — What You're Testing & What to Watch For

The core question: Does naming a specific action (Version A) change behavior more than just naming the pattern (Version B)? Or do people who only see the data figure out what to do on their own?

What Version A assumes: Visibility is necessary but not sufficient. Noor already knows she overspends — what she lacks is a concrete next action. The suggestion converts awareness into a micro-commitment she can actually hold.

What Version B assumes: The data itself is the intervention. Once people see the payday pattern named clearly, they recognize themselves in it and self-direct. Adding a suggestion is either unnecessary or mildly patronizing.

What to listen for in follow-ups:

  • Version A people: Did they follow the specific suggestion, or did they do something else instead? (If they did something different, the suggestion was still a trigger — just not the exact action.)
  • Version B people: Did they do nothing, or did they spontaneously name their own rule? (If they made up their own suggestion, visibility alone may be enough.)
  • Both groups: Did anyone push back on the data? ("That doesn't sound right" or "I already knew that.") Noor's self-image and her actual behavior are misaligned — some recipients may reject the mirror rather than change in response to it.

The answer that most changes your product direction:
If Version B people change behavior at the same rate as Version A, build the visibility tool. If Version A people change at a meaningfully higher rate, the suggestion layer is load-bearing and you're building a habit coach. If nobody changes in either group, neither hypothesis is right and the intervention point is somewhere else entirely.
