# Business Model Canvas

Built iteratively. Each block added only when grounded in interview and competitive evidence.

## Block 1: Customer Segments

**Primary segment:** Recent college graduates who are newly financially independent and struggling to consistently manage their money.

The target customer has income and real financial obligations — student loans, rent, credit cards — but no working system for managing them. They are not oblivious. They know roughly what they should do. The gap is between knowing and consistently following through.

**Two candidate filters, both open:**

*Filter A — No system.* The person has never built a tracking mechanism. They manage on intention and self-monitoring. They have not tried budgeting tools seriously, or never started. The product's job is to give them a starting point that requires no setup.

*Filter B — Knows but can't follow through.* The person has enough knowledge to act but something interrupts consistent execution. They may have tried tracking before. They resolve to change after a bad month and the cycle repeats anyway. The product's job is to interrupt that cycle at the moment of resolution.

Noor (Interview 2) fits both. Tamara (Interview 3) fits Filter A specifically, not both — she has never created a personal budget, has always relied on someone else (her mother) to manage money on her behalf, and reports no anxiety about overspending, consistent with someone who has not yet built self-monitoring habits rather than someone who has it and can't act on it. Two target-customer interviews with two different filter profiles is a real signal: this may not be one segment, it may be two.

**What puts someone outside this segment:**
- Already has a working system, even a self-built one (Ben, Interview 1: two accounts, Bank of America tools, fully in control)
- Actively using a budgeting app
- Not yet financially independent — employment and real obligations are the trigger, not graduation itself

**Evidence base:** Two target-customer interviews (Noor, 24; Tamara, 24). Their profiles diverge on which filter applies, which is itself useful information — it suggests the segment isn't uniform. Still short of the three-to-five interview threshold needed to treat either filter, or the segment as a whole, as validated.

## Block 2: Value Propositions

**Core value:** Automatic spending awareness for people who are newly financially independent — delivered before they are asked to budget, not instead of it.

No current competitor addresses the step before budgeting. YNAB, Monarch Money, and EveryDollar all require setup and assume the user is ready to manage their money. The proposed value is explicitly earlier: help the target customer understand their current habits with no manual effort, so that when they resolve to change, there is something specific to act on.

**What the evidence supports:**

*Removing the friction of self-review.* Noor has no structured picture of her spending. After overspending, her response is vague — "try to be more careful" — because there is nothing concrete to return to. Automatic categorization gives her something specific without requiring her to build it. This directly addresses the manual effort that competitor reviews consistently name as the top reason users drop off (YNAB: setup effort; EveryDollar: manual entry; Monarch Money: categorization effort).

*A concrete reference point at the moment of resolution.* Noor's pattern — spending surprise, vague resolve, repeat — suggests the missing piece is not the realization but what happens after it. A regular, plain-language summary of where money went gives a user something to look at in the moment they decide to do better, rather than relying on willpower alone.

*No budget required to start.* The beachhead position is the gap no competitor occupies: financial clarity before financial management.

**What is still assumed, not yet validated:**

- That users will connect a bank account to a new product. Neither interviewee was asked this, and neither described wanting a tool. Willingness to share financial data is untested.
- That awareness — even automatic, frictionless awareness — is sufficient to interrupt the cycle. The assumption scorecard rates this as partially confirmed with a significant caveat: Noor already has general awareness and still cycles. Whether structured, regular delivery changes that is an open question.
- That AI-generated plain-language explanations are the right mechanism. This had zero customer validation through the first two interviews. Interview 3 (Tamara) is the first to react to it directly, and the reaction was positive — but she also named ChatGPT Plus, which she already uses, as a substitute for similar help. That means the AI framing needs to differentiate itself from a general-purpose AI assistant, not just claim the AI label, to justify the $5–8/month she said she'd pay.

**The value proposition that is supportable right now:**
Automatic, no-setup spending awareness for people who are newly financially independent — delivered before they are asked to budget.

**The value proposition that is still a hypothesis:**
That delivering this awareness regularly, in plain language, is what interrupts the cycle of surprise and vague resolve — and that users will trust and engage with an AI-connected product to get it.

## Block 5: Revenue Streams

**What the interviews tell us:** For Noor and Ben, nothing directly — neither was asked about payment history or willingness to pay. Interview 3 (Tamara) changes this partially: when the product concept was described to her directly, she said she'd pay $5–8/month, and named the passive, no-manual-input design as the reason. She also mentioned she could ask ChatGPT Plus — which she already pays for — for similar help, meaning the comparison point in her head isn't a free competitor, it's a subscription she already holds. This is the first real willingness-to-pay signal in the project. It is one person, reacting to a hypothetical, on the consumer side only — it says nothing about institutional pricing, and one data point cannot set a price.

**What the competitive landscape tells us:**

This is the only evidence base available right now.

| Competitor | Pricing Model | Price Point |
|---|---|---|
| YNAB | Subscription | ~$109/year |
| Monarch Money | Subscription | ~$100/year |
| EveryDollar | Freemium | Free + paid tier |
| Rocket Money | Freemium | Free + paid tier |
| Credit Karma | Free (referral/affiliate) | $0 to user |

User complaints in the competitive analysis flag "upsells" (Rocket Money) and "upgrade prompts" (EveryDollar) as friction points. That suggests the target segment notices and reacts to aggressive monetization, but it does not tell us what they are willing to pay or whether they would pay at all.

**Revenue model options — none validated:**

*B2C subscription.* Direct charge to the user on a monthly or annual basis. Sits within the market range established by competitors. Straightforward model. Tamara's stated $5–8/month is the first evidence that at least one target customer would consider this — though a stated price for a hypothetical is a much weaker signal than an actual payment, and this remains one person. What is still untested: whether the target customer has ever paid for a financial tool, whether they would pay before experiencing value, and whether this product can hold a subscriber long enough to recoup acquisition cost.

*Freemium.* Core awareness value delivered free. Additional features — coaching, goal tracking, deeper insights, historical trends — on a paid tier. Reduces the barrier to initial adoption. What is untested: whether the free tier creates enough value to justify upgrading, and whether the product can define a paid tier that is genuinely compelling rather than just gated features.

*B2B2C through employers.* Employer pays for financial wellness as a benefit; the target customer receives the product free. Removes willingness-to-pay as a variable entirely for the user. What is untested: whether employers in the relevant segment offer this category of benefit, what they pay for it, and whether the sales motion is feasible at an early stage.

*B2B2C through universities.* Same logic as employer model, applied to financial aid offices or student success programs. What is untested: budget availability, procurement timelines, and whether universities would position this as a graduation transition resource.

*Referral or affiliate.* Free to users; revenue generated by recommending financial products — savings accounts, loan refinancing, credit cards. Removes price as a barrier for the user. What is untested: whether this model can generate sufficient revenue at the volume this venture would reach early on, and whether it creates an incentive misalignment between the product's stated purpose (financial health) and how it makes money.

**What needs to be tested before choosing a model:**

The most important missing data point is not which model is structurally best — it is whether Noor would pay anything at all, and if so, what outcome would justify it. The next interviews should ask: what financial tools or services do you currently pay for, if any? What happened the last time you tried a free financial app? What would a tool have to do for you to consider paying for it? Those answers exist nowhere in the current evidence base.

## Block 6: Cost Structure

**What we know with reasonable confidence:**

*Bank data aggregation.* Connecting to a user's bank account requires a data aggregation service. Plaid is the market standard. It charges per connected account, which means this cost scales directly with users. At low volume it is manageable; at scale it is a significant cost that the revenue model must cover. This cost is unavoidable if the product connects to bank accounts — it is not a design choice.

*AI and language model infrastructure.* Generating plain-language summaries of spending data requires LLM API usage, which is priced per token. This cost also scales with usage. It can be kept lean early by batching requests and caching outputs, but it grows with the user base.

*Engineering for bank connection maintenance.* Bank APIs change, aggregators have outages, and data formats shift. Maintaining reliable bank connections is an ongoing engineering cost, not a one-time build. This is a category where maintenance is chronic.

*Compliance infrastructure.* Handling financial data carries non-optional compliance requirements: SOC 2 certification, data privacy policy and enforcement, and potentially licensing depending on what the product does with the data. These are fixed costs that arrive before revenue does.

**What we suspect but have not confirmed:**

The unit economics of this model depend heavily on the ratio between bank aggregation cost per user and revenue per user. If the product is free to users and monetized through a B2B2C model, the aggregation cost must be covered by institutional revenue. If it is a freemium model, free users generate cost without generating revenue, which means the paid conversion rate is load-bearing. Neither of these ratios has been calculated because revenue per user is untested.

**What we do not know:**

Actual cost at operating scale. Early-stage estimates for aggregation and AI infrastructure are available from public pricing, but they shift with negotiated volume agreements and product architecture choices that have not been made yet. Cost structure at this stage is a list of known cost categories, not a model.

## Block 3: Channels

**Evidence rating: WEAK. No interview data on how target customers discover tools or seek financial information. Everything below is inferred from the target customer profile.**

Neither Noor nor Ben was asked where they look for financial information, how they have discovered tools in the past, or what prompted any financial decision they have made. Noor is not in solution-seeking mode — her response to overspending is a private resolution, not a search. She is unlikely to find this product through the App Store or a Google search. The channel has to reach her before she knows she is looking.

**The life-stage trigger as the channel logic:**

The problem activates at a specific convergence: first job, moving out, student loan repayment beginning. Noor named all three. Channels that intercept people at that moment — before they know they need something — are more likely to work than channels that depend on active intent.

*University career centers and financial aid offices.* Present at the graduation transition, when students encounter loan repayment, first salaries, and first leases in a short window.

*Employer onboarding.* First jobs are a parallel trigger. Some employers offer financial wellness as a benefit. This channel does not require a download decision from the user.

*Student loan servicers.* Servicers contact borrowers at repayment start — exactly when the target customer is managing money independently for the first time.

*Short-form financial content.* Personal finance content for people in their 20s has significant organic reach on TikTok and Instagram. Content that delivers value in-feed builds familiarity without requiring any action. This is inferred from the demographic, not from either interview.

**What needs to be tested:** Where Noor goes when she has a money question — if anywhere. How she discovered the last app she downloaded. Whether she follows any financial content. None of this exists in the interview record.

## Block 4: Customer Relationships

**Evidence rating: WEAK. The proactive framing is inferred from one interviewee's behavioral pattern. Neither interviewee was asked what kind of relationship they would want with a financial tool.**

**What the interviews suggest, indirectly:**

Noor's cycle — spending surprise, vague resolve, repeat — breaks because there is nothing to return to after the moment of resolve fades. A passive tool she has to remember to open will not interrupt that. Her magic fix reinforces this: "To shop smarter. I definitely need to re-evaluate purchases and appreciate what I already have." That is not a dashboard request. It points toward a coaching relationship — something that shows up regularly and holds her connected to a decision she already made.

**Relationship model suggested by the evidence:**

*Proactive and recurring.* A weekly or monthly summary pushed to the user — not waiting for her to log in — gives her something concrete at the moment of resolve rather than relying on willpower alone.

*Low-accountability to start.* Neither interviewee expressed readiness for intensive engagement. Heavy requirements before delivering value risk replicating the onboarding friction that causes competitors to lose users.

**What is assumed:** That users will engage with proactively delivered content rather than ignoring it. That automated delivery is preferable to a human relationship. That recurring summaries are preferable to on-demand access. None of these has been tested.

**What needs to be tested:** "If something sent you a weekly summary of your spending, would you read it?" "What kind of financial check-in would feel helpful versus annoying?" "Have you ever used an app that sent you regular updates — what did you do with them?"

## Block 7: Key Resources

**Evidence rating: WEAK. Resources follow from a product concept that is itself unvalidated. The most critical resource — user trust — has never been tested.**

*Bank data access.* Via a data aggregation service (Plaid is the market standard). This is a structural requirement if the product connects to bank accounts. Without it, the core value proposition — automatic categorization — does not exist.

*AI and language model capability.* Required to generate plain-language summaries from transaction data. Available via API; cost scales with usage.

*Proprietary categorization logic.* The accuracy and usefulness of spending summaries depends on how well transactions are categorized. This is what differentiates a summary from a bank statement. It is built, not bought, and improves over time with data.

*User trust — willingness to connect a bank account.* This was the hardest resource to acquire and the one with the weakest evidence — until Interview 3. Tamara was asked directly and did not refuse, but was not immediately comfortable either: she cited limited financial knowledge, said she would consult her mother or boyfriend before connecting any account, and said she'd need to see verification, reviews, and a recognizably trustworthy source (she named Amazon as the kind of brand trust that would help) before connecting. This is the first direct evidence on the most critical unknown in the entire model. It suggests the barrier is real friction, not a flat refusal — trust-building signals (third-party verification, visible reviews, a credible brand presence) may be able to address it, though this is one person's stated reaction, not observed behavior at the actual connection step.

*Founder credibility and lived experience.* The WHYME document establishes that the founder has lived the problem — survived on a low income, educated herself out of debt, experienced the cycle firsthand. This is a genuine differentiation from a founder who is theorizing about the problem. It is the one resource that exists right now without needing to be built or bought.

## Block 8: Key Activities

**Evidence rating: WEAK. Activities follow from product design decisions that are unvalidated. Which activities are most important depends on revenue model and channel, both of which remain open.**

*Building and maintaining bank connections.* Bank APIs change, aggregators have outages, and data formats shift. This is ongoing engineering work, not a one-time build. It begins before the product can be used and never stops.

*Categorization and summary generation.* The core daily activity of the product: ingesting transaction data, categorizing it accurately, and producing plain-language output. Quality here is what the value proposition depends on.

*Push delivery on a cadence.* If the relationship model is proactive — summaries sent to users rather than pulled by them — delivering consistently and reliably is itself a key activity. Timing, format, and frequency all affect whether users engage or ignore.

*Customer or institutional acquisition.* Depends entirely on the revenue model chosen. B2C requires consumer marketing. B2B2C requires institutional sales. These are different activities with different skill requirements and timelines.

*Compliance maintenance.* Handling financial data requires ongoing attention to SOC 2, data privacy, and any regulatory changes affecting fintech products. This is not a launch task — it is a permanent operational cost.

## Block 9: Key Partnerships

**Evidence rating: WEAK. Partnership needs follow from revenue model and channel choices, both of which are unvalidated. No interviewee mentioned any partner organization.**

*Bank data aggregator — structural dependency.* Plaid or a comparable service is required regardless of which revenue model or channel is chosen. This partnership is not optional if the product connects to bank accounts. It is the one partnership that must be established before anything else can be built.

*If B2B2C through employers: HR and benefits platforms.* Employers deliver benefits through platforms like Benefitfocus, Businessolver, or directly through HR teams. Reaching employers at scale requires either direct sales or integration with existing benefits infrastructure.

*If B2B2C through universities: financial aid offices and student success programs.* These relationships require direct outreach and procurement processes that move slowly. The advantage is that universities already have trust with the target customer at the exact trigger moment.

*If B2B2C through student loan servicers: loan servicer partnerships.* Servicers contact borrowers at repayment start. A partnership here creates a channel and a distribution mechanism simultaneously.

*If affiliate model: financial product providers.* Credit unions, savings account providers, and refinancing companies pay for qualified referrals. This requires enough user volume to be worth the partnership and creates incentive alignment questions that need to be examined before committing to the model.

## Interview To-Do List

Every block flagged as WEAK above represents a gap in the evidence. These are the questions the next round of interviews must answer. Until they are, the canvas is a set of hypotheses, not a validated model.

**Highest priority — structural risks:**

1. "Would you connect your bank account to a new app you had just discovered? What would make you comfortable or uncomfortable with that?" *(Tests Block 7: Key Resources — user trust. If the answer is no, the product concept requires redesign.)*

2. "Walk me through the last time you tried to manage your spending or change a money habit. What did you try? What happened?" *(Tests Block 1: Customer Segments — Filter A vs. B. Tells you whether the segment has no system or has tried and failed.)*

3. "What financial tools or services do you currently pay for, if any? What would a product have to do for you to consider paying for it?" *(Tests Block 5: Revenue Streams — willingness to pay has zero interview evidence.)*

**Second priority — product and relationship design:**

4. "If you found out today that you spent $400 on food last month when you thought you spent $200 — what would you do with that information?" *(Tests Block 2: Value Propositions — whether awareness produces action.)*

5. "If something sent you a weekly summary of your spending with no setup required, would you read it? What would make you open it or ignore it?" *(Tests Block 4: Customer Relationships — whether proactive delivery actually works.)*

6. "Have you ever heard of or used any AI tools for money — in a banking app, a budgeting tool, or otherwise? What did you think?" *(Tests Block 2: Value Propositions — the AI mechanism is the most assumed element in the canvas.)*

**Third priority — channel and discovery:**

7. "Where do you go when you have a money question — or do you go anywhere at all?" *(Tests Block 3: Channels — no interview data on discovery behavior exists.)*

8. "How did you find the last app you actually kept using?" *(Tests Block 3: Channels — reveals real discovery behavior, not stated preference.)*
